Method C — Physical plug (hardware jacks)

Twenty-six 3.5 mm jacks, one per letter A–Z, are wired directly to GPIO pins on the MSP432E401Y with internal pull-ups. Mono patch cables connect any two jacks to create a substitution pair. Up to 13 simultaneous pairs are tracked in real time.

Hardware wiring guide

  • 1
    Use mono (TS) cables, not stereo (TRS)
    Male-to-male mono 3.5 mm cables only. Stereo cables have a third conductor that will corrupt the scan. Mono cables have one signal conductor and one ground sleeve — both ends act identically as a short.
  • 2
    Mount 26 panel-mount mono jacks
    Use panel-mount normally-closed mono 3.5 mm jacks. Connect the switch terminal of each jack to its GPIO pin per the table above. Connect all jack ground (sleeve) terminals to a shared GND rail.
  • 3
    Verify polarity before connecting all 26
    With no plug inserted the jack switch is closed — it pulls the pin LOW through GND. With a plug inserted the switch opens and the pin floats HIGH via WPU. Open PuTTY and verify one jack before wiring the rest.
  • 4
    Connect UART for debug output
    USB-to-serial adapter: PA0 (RX), PA1 (TX), shared GND. Open terminal at 115 200 baud, 8N1. The board prints a startup banner on every reset followed by the full pair state after each event.
  • 5
    Test all 5 transitions before full use
    Insert one end of a cable (T1). Insert the other end (T2). To test T3 and T4 individually you need two single-ended cables. A complete cable always removes both ends simultaneously (T4+T5 sequence).

Section 12

Enigma Machine Reference — Rotors, Reflectors & Wiring

This section documents the internal mechanics of the Enigma cipher engine. Understanding the hardware analogy helps when reading PuTTY traces and diagnosing unexpected encryption output.

12a — What is a rotor?

A rotor (German: Walze) is a disc with 26 electrical contacts on each face. Each contact on the entry face is wired to a different contact on the exit face, forming a fixed monoalphabetic substitution. In software, this is a 26-element lookup table.

Three rotors sit in a row — Left (L), Middle (M), Right (R). Every keypress steps the right rotor by one position before the electrical signal passes through. The signal travels right-to-left through all three rotors, bounces off the reflector, then travels left-to-right back. The return path uses the same wiring traversed backwards — making the cipher self-inverse.

Self-inverse property: If pressing A produces X in ENCRYPT mode, then pressing X produces A with the same settings. The firmware uses the exact same enigma_encrypt_char() function for both directions.

12d — Reflectors (Umkehrwalze)

The reflector sits fixed at the left end of the rotor stack. It maps every letter to a unique partner and is always symmetric — if A→Y then Y→A — and no letter ever maps to itself. These two properties make the cipher self-inverse and make it impossible to encrypt a letter as itself (a weakness Bletchley Park exploited). The reflector does not step.

12f — The double-step anomaly

The Enigma's ratchet-and-pawl mechanism differs from a simple odometer. On each keypress: the right rotor always steps; the middle steps when the right was at its turnover position; the left steps when the middle is at its own turnover — but this check happens before the middle moves, so both the middle and left step simultaneously. Section 14

The three-part formula — every term explained

Every rotor substitution — forward or reverse — uses the same three-step sequence. The rotor's wiring is fixed inside the drum, but the drum has rotated, so you must convert the incoming letter into the drum's own coordinate system before looking it up, then convert the result back out.

Step A — shift input INTO the drum's frame of reference
entry= (input + position − ring + 26) mod 26
Step B — look up the fixed internal wiring
raw= wiring[entry]    ← index into the 26-element table
Step C — shift result BACK to the fixed alphabet frame
output= (raw − position + ring + 26) mod 26

Section 15

Why +26 appears in the formula

Modulo of a negative number behaves differently in C than in mathematics. In C, (-1) % 26 evaluates to -1, not 25. The formula needs the result to be 0–25 — a negative remainder would produce an illegal array index and crash the firmware.

Adding 26 before the mod guarantees the value inside the parentheses is always ≥ 0 for any valid input (0–25), position (0–25), and ring (0–25).

Worst case — minimum possible value before +26
entry= (0 + 0 − 25 + 26) mod 26 = 1 mod 26 = 1 ✓
Without +26 — negative result crashes the firmware
entry= (0 + 0 − 25) mod 26 = −25 mod 26 = −25 ✗ (invalid index)
The +26 never changes the result. Adding 26 to a number before taking mod 26 is mathematically identical to not adding it — 26 mod 26 = 0, so the extra 26 vanishes into the remainder. Its sole purpose is preventing a negative value from reaching the wiring table index in C.

Section 26

How the complete system operates — start to finish

The system intercepts the Brother SX-4000 typewriter at the hardware level. The typewriter's keyboard and its print mechanism are physically separated at their FPC connectors. The MSP432E401Y LaunchPad sits in between, reading every key pressed on the keyboard and deciding what character to send to the printer — either the same character (PASSTHROUGH), or an Enigma-transformed character (ENCRYPT/DECRYPT).

The system has three distinct hardware domains connected through the MSP432:

Domain 1 — Typewriter keyboard: 16 signal lines (8 columns + 8 rows) come from the keyboard FPC into MSP GPIO inputs. The MSP actively scans these by driving each row LOW one at a time and reading which column goes LOW through a pressed key.
Domain 2 — MSP432 processing core: Detects the key, looks up the letter, optionally transforms it through the Enigma cipher engine, then encodes the output letter as a 3-bit column CBA and 3-bit row CBA address.
Domain 3 — Typewriter printer: Two CD4051BE 8-channel analog multiplexers receive the CBA address lines. A CD4066BD bilateral switch (enable line PB3) gates the injection. When the MSP drives the CBA outputs and the enable is HIGH, the printer sees a matrix key event and prints the character.

Complete signal flow — one keypress

1KEY PRESSED→Row + col shorted inside typewriter keyboard
2SCAN→MSP drives rows LOW one at a time, reads col — active col reads LOW
3DECODE→KB_KEYS[row][col] → letter A–Z
4aPASSTHROUGH→output = input, LCD shows same letter on both lines
4bENCRYPT/DEC→enigma_encrypt_char_traced() → output letter, LCD shows in/out
5ENCODE CBA→OUTPUT_COL[letter] and OUTPUT_ROW[letter] → 3-bit binary addresses
6INJECT→PK0-2 (col CBA) + PK3/PA4-5 (row CBA) driven. PB3 always HIGH.
7PRINTER→CD4051 muxes select col and row. Typewriter prints the output letter.

scan_once() is called twice with a 20 ms gap. If both calls return the same letter, the key is considered stable. The wait-for-release is intentionally left to the main loop — get_key() returns immediately with the key still held so the injection can hold as long as the physical key is pressed.

PD7 NMI unlock

PD7 (row 4) is the Non-Maskable Interrupt pin and is hardware-locked at reset. The firmware writes the unlock key (0x4C4F434B = ASCII "LOCK") to the GPIOLOCK register, sets bit 7 in GPIOCR to permit reconfiguration, then re-locks. Without this three-line sequence, every GPIOPinWrite to PD7 is silently ignored.

HWREG(GPIO_PORTD_BASE + 0x520) = 0x4C4F434B; // write unlock key
HWREG(GPIO_PORTD_BASE + 0x524) |= GPIO_PIN_7; // allow PD7 change
HWREG(GPIO_PORTD_BASE + 0x520) = 0; // re-lock

Section 44

Main loop and complete signal flow per keypress

Keyboard scan — active row-drive

get_key() calls scan_once() twice with a 20 ms gap. scan_once() drives each of the 8 rows LOW one at a time, waits 20 µs for signal settling, then reads all 8 column lines. A column that reads LOW while its row is active means a key is pressed at that intersection. KB_KEYS[row][col] returns the letter. Two consecutive matching readings confirm the press. get_key() returns without waiting for release so the key is still physically held when the result arrives.

process_key() — the three paths

1PASSTHROUGH→out = key_in → set_cba(OUTPUT_COL[out], OUTPUT_ROW[out]) → same letter injected
2ENCRYPT→out = enigma_encrypt_char_traced(key_in) → set_cba(OUTPUT_COL[out], OUTPUT_ROW[out])
3DECRYPT→same function as ENCRYPT — Enigma is self-inverse
4SPACE/RET→always passthrough regardless of mode → fixed CBA values

Injection hold — tied to physical key

After set_cba() drives the output pins, the firmware enters a polling loop: while(scan_once() != '\0') delay_ms(1). The CBA lines remain driven and PM0 LED stays ON for as long as the key is physically held. When the key is released, scan_once() returns null, clear_cba() zeroes all output pins, PM0 goes LOW, and [INJECT OFF] is sent to PuTTY. This ties the injection duration exactly to the physical key press duration — the typewriter's controller detects the matrix event in its next scan cycle regardless of timing.

Return key — message save and rotor reset

When Return is pressed, it is injected with its fixed CBA values exactly like any other key. After the injection clears, the firmware checks if msg_len > 0. If so, the accumulated plaintext and ciphertext buffers are saved to the control panel history log with a mode tag (P, E, or D), the rotors are reset to their Grundstellung (daily starting positions), g_typing_started is cleared, and a separator is printed to PuTTY. The next key press begins a fresh message.

Circuit interconnection description

FPC1 — keyboard to MSP432 (scan input path)

The typewriter keyboard's FPC1 connector is intercepted. Sixteen wires from FPC1 are routed to the MSP432 LaunchPad header pins: 8 column lines to PE4, PC4, PC5, PC6, PE5, PD3, PC7, PB2 and 8 row lines to PE0–PE3, PD7, PM1, PM4, PM5. The MSP drives each row LOW in sequence (active row-drive scan). The column lines idle HIGH through internal WPU resistors. A pressed key shorts its row to its column, pulling the column LOW and triggering detection.

FPC2 — MSP432 to printer (injection output path)

FPC2 connects the typewriter's main controller board (the print side). Six CBA output lines from the MSP432 (PK0-PK3, PA4, PA5) drive the address inputs of two CD4051BE 8-channel analog multiplexers. One CD4051 selects the column line; the other selects the row line. The selected col output and selected row output are connected together through one switch of the CD4066BD bilateral switch. The CD4066 enable pin (PB3) is wired permanently HIGH. When the MSP drives a valid col/row CBA address, the two selected FPC2 lines are shorted together, simulating a key press at that matrix position. The typewriter's controller detects this in its next scan cycle and prints the corresponding character.

Control panel wiring

Five push-buttons connect each button's one leg to a GPIO pin (PD2, PQ0, PP4, PN5, PN4) and the other leg to GND. The internal WPU resistors hold the pins HIGH at rest; pressing the button pulls the pin LOW. The two LEDs connect through 330Ω resistors from PB4 and PB5 to GND. The keypress LED connects through 330Ω from PM0 to GND.

LCD connection

The NHD-0420D3Z LCD connects its RX pin to PP1 (UART6 TX). Power (5V or 3.3V depending on display version) and GND complete the connection. No RX from the LCD is used — the driver is transmit-only. Commands are sent with a 0xFE prefix byte followed by the command byte. Text is sent as raw ASCII bytes.

Physical plugboard

26 normally-closed 3.5mm mono jacks are each wired with their tip contact to one GPIO pin and their sleeve (ring) contact to GND. The NC contact inside each empty jack shorts tip to GND, pulling the pin LOW. When a cable is inserted, it mechanically opens the NC contact; the WPU resistor pulls the pin HIGH. When a cable connects two jacks, the drive-LOW scan detects the pair: driving one HIGH jack LOW causes the other to follow if they are connected through a cable.

PD7 NMI unlock: PD7 (row 4 scan line) is the Non-Maskable Interrupt pin, hardware-locked at reset. The firmware writes the unlock key 0x4C4F434B to GPIOLOCK register (offset 0x520), sets bit 7 in GPIOCR (offset 0x524), then re-locks. Without this sequence, all writes to PD7 are silently ignored. GPIO_O_LOCK and GPIO_O_CR are not defined in the MSP432E4 SDK — raw offsets must be used.
PM1 row reassignment: Row 5 (letters E N O Q T U V and Return) was originally assigned to PD6. PD6 was found to be unreliable on this hardware instance — the scan was not triggering correctly. Row 5 was reassigned to PM1 (J8 connector pin 2). This required updating SCAN_D_MASK to remove GPIO_PIN_6, adding GPIO_PIN_1 to SCAN_M_MASK, updating ROW_BASE[5] from GPIO_PORTD_BASE to GPIO_PORTM_BASE, and updating ROW_PIN[5] from GPIO_PIN_6 to GPIO_PIN_1.
